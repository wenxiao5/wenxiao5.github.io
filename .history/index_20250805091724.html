On the Role and Design of Resale Royalties2025
with Zijun (June) Shi and Song Lin
Abstract: Resale markets span diverse sectors from physical goods to digital assets, with resale royalties evolving into varied forms through advances in platform economics and blockchain technology. This paper examines how resale royalty policies should be designed under varying degrees of information asymmetry between creators and buyers. Based on whether creators have the decision power to set and adjust royalty rates, we classify royalty policies into three types: exogenous, adjustable, and committed. With a unifying framework, we analyze how different policies, and how a designer's (platform or regulator) control through royalty ceiling, can influence the equilibrium behaviors and welfare implications. Our analysis yields four principal findings. First, we identify a fundamental trade-off in royalty mechanisms: while royalties mitigate adverse selection and price distortion problems in primary markets, they generate secondary market deadweight losses, rendering royalty policies welfare-improving only under moderate information asymmetry. Second, committed policies enable dual signaling through price and royalty rate, enhancing effectiveness compared to other policies. High-quality creators prioritize royalty-based signaling to minimize price distortion, consistently leading to royalty rates exceeding socially optimal levels, making restrictive ceilings socially beneficial. Third, restrictive ceilings harm high-quality creators in separating equilibria by constraining signaling space, but benefit them in pooling equilibria by lowering maximum royalty rates necessary to prevent deviations. Fourth, committed and exogenous royalty policies generally dominate adjustable policies from the perspectives of both high-quality creators and social welfare, while low-quality creators generally prefer no royalty. These insights provide actionable guidance for policymakers and platform designers navigating modern resale ecosystems.
Strategic Disinformation Generation and Detection2025-- Major revision at Management Science
with Yunfei (Jesse) Yao and Pengxiang Zhou
Abstract: Disinformation detection is becoming increasingly important and relevant because it is easier than ever to create and disseminate disinformation. How does detection ability affect the incentive to generate disinformation? Given the practical constraints of classification technology, how should a detector be designed? To answer these questions, this paper studies the problem where a sender strategically communicates his type (high or low) to a receiver, and a lie detector generates a noisy signal on the truthfulness of the sender's message. The receiver then infers the sender's type both through the message from the sender and through the signal from the detector. We find a non-monotonic relationship between the probability that the low-type sender is lying and the accuracy of detection. More accurate detection (a higher true-positive rate and a lower false-positive rate) increases the probability of lying when the true-positive rate is low, because of a persuasive effect. By contrast, more accurate detection decreases the probability of lying when the true-positive rate is high, because of a dissuasive effect. We also characterize the optimal detector design. The designer always chooses the lowest feasible false-positive rate for any true-positive rate. The possibility of false-positive alarms implies that the designer chooses an intermediate true-positive rate rather than the highest true-positive rate. Counter-intuitively, the optimal detector may raise an alarm about a smaller percentage of disinformation when its underlying classifier is better at distinguishing the sender's type.
